Anomie Theory

Anomie theory is the theory that crime is caused by disillusionment between people and social norms, and that states of "normlessness" or lack of social regulation leads to crime.

"Anomie in a general sense refers to weaknesses in the normative order of a society" (Source - Messner and Rosenfeld)

Strain Theory

Merton's strain theory proposes that crime comes from the differential between goals or values which are pedestalized within a society and the unavailability of those goals to the average person. For example, if wealth is a valued societal trait in a capitalistic country but is not attainable to the average person through realistic means, they may resort to crime to achieve these goals. Strain theory proposes 5 different modes of adaptation to these differentials, and suggests that the innovator mode is the most likely to commit crime, searching for societal value via unconventional means. Crime can be caused by an expectation differential; when a person is deprived of a resource that is culturally valued by their society, yet finds a differential between their and others' access to it, they may find criminal ways to pursue this resource.

Durkheim

Durkheim argued that crime is a normal, healthy part of functioning societies that is bound to exist as long as societies do because societies naturally contain disagreement and that crime is societies ways of punishing wrongdoers. This theory is consistent with mala prohibita, punishment of acts that are prohibited simply because they are bad in nature.

Institutional Anomie Theory

Institutional anomie theory posits that there are four main institutions of society that require balance; the economy, family, education, and government. When the institution of the economy dominates every other one of these institutions in every way in hypercapitalistic societies like America, the social control that the other 3 institutions have weakened and causes anomie and strain, which leads to crime. This could also apply if one of the other 3 institutions dominated rather than the economy.

Differential Association

Social disorganization and learning theory as well as differential association posits that crime is passed down among conditions inside relationships, and among people which is influenced by weakened informal control inside of said communities and increases likelihood of both perpetration and victimization.

Social Control Theory

Social control posits that a variety of factors within society, including institutions, people, and social factors influence the informal social control a society holds over itself. By this theory, the more control a society has, the less it experiences crime through things like anomie and strain.
