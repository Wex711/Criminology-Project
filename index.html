About Crime

Crime is often believed to be a product of individual decisions, and in our neoliberalistic society it is a common belief that a person is the sole determinant of their actions. However, there are greater factors at play; social structures and environments shape the way people approach their problems, and factors like social inequality, community conditions, institutional failures and others amplify these approaches. Cultural pressures and macrocosms like anomie and strain only make these approaches more tempting. In this website, I will cover how different factors influence why and how people commit crimes and how we can prevent crime by tackling the problems around it before they begin.

Crime is rampant in the United States with approximately 1,325,584 violent crimes and 6,513,815 property crimes being committed annually, with the incidence rate for violent crime being 398.5 per 100,000 and property crime being 1,958.2 per 100,000.

Source: Federal Bureau of Investigation. (2020). Crime Data Explorer.

What Causes Crime

Classical Theory

People have free will and crime happens because people go through a series of measurements, including benefit/cost measurements, pleasure/pain measurements, and crime becomes most attractive when these measurements favor the perpetrator. Offenders consider the likelihood of success, arrest, reward, and punishment. Large proponents of this theory include Cesare Beccaria and Jeremy Bentham. Under this theory, crime is better prevented than punished.

"It is better to prevent crimes than to punish them."

Source: Beccaria, 1764/1963

Anomie Theory

Anomie theory is the theory that crime is caused by disillusionment between people and social norms, and that states of "normlessness" or lack of social regulation lead to crime.

"Anomie in a general sense refers to weaknesses in the normative order of a society."

Source: Messner & Rosenfeld

Strain Theory

Merton's strain theory proposes that crime comes from the differential between goals or values which are pedestalized within a society and the unavailability of those goals to the average person. For example, if wealth is a valued societal trait in a capitalistic country but is not attainable to the average person through realistic means, they may resort to crime to achieve these goals.

Strain theory proposes five different modes of adaptation to these differentials, and suggests that the innovator mode is the most likely to commit crime, searching for societal value via unconventional means. Crime can be caused by an expectation differential; when a person is deprived of a resource that is culturally valued by their society, yet finds a differential between their and others' access to it, they may find criminal ways to pursue this resource.

Why Do People Commit Crime

Durkheim

Durkheim argued that crime is a normal, healthy part of functioning societies that is bound to exist as long as societies do because societies naturally contain disagreement and that crime is society's way of punishing wrongdoers.

This theory is consistent with mala prohibita, punishment of acts that are prohibited simply because they are bad in nature.

How to Prevent Crime

Rational Choice Theory

Rational choice theory argues that crime is preventable through methods of implementing prevention methods like cameras, locks, new technologies, and more. Through the pleasure/pain principle, increasing the risks and reducing the rewards of crime can discourage offenders and prevent criminal behavior.
