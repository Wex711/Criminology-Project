This website looks at the causes of crime and what factors that influence it through a theoretical and applied lens, and takes the approach that prevention is possible for a majority of crime through certain approached rather than a reaction based approach, which is how crime is typically approached in our existing society.

Disclaimer - When Crime Isn’t Justifiable

This website's point is that crime comes from many places and is often a result of things like institutional and societal factors, but it is an important distinction that this does not excuse crime. While lots of crime is socially constructed, lots of crime displays mala prohibita and can produce real harm and violate social order. By looking at the cause of crime, my goal is not to excuse it but to find causes and look for places where prevention can be developed.

Crime Has Victims

Crime is rampant in the United States with approximately 1,325,584 violent crimes and 6,513,815 property crimes being committed annually, with the incidence rate for violent crime being 398.5 per 100,000 and property crime being 1,958.2 per 100,000. (Source - Federal Bureau of Investigation. (2020). Crime Data Explorer)

Crime Is Complex

Crime is often believed to be a product of individual decisions, and in our neoliberalistic society it is a common belief that a person is the sole determinant of their actions. However, there are greater factors at play; social structures and environments shape the way people approach their problems, and factors like social inequality, community conditions, institutional failures and others amplify these approaches.

Classical Theory

People have free will and crime happens because people go through a series of measurements, including benefit/cost measurements, pleasure/pain measurements, and that crime becomes most attractive when these measurements favor the perpetrator. Offenders consider likelihood of success, arrest, reward and punishment. Large proponents of this theory include Cessare Bekaria and Jeremy Bentham. Under this theory, crime is better prevented than punished.

"It is better to prevent crimes than to punish them." (Source - Beccaria, 1764/1963)

Transition

Cultural pressures and macrocosms like anomie and strain only make these approaches more tempting. In this website, I will cover how different factors influence why and how people commit which crimes and how we can prevent crimes by tackling the problems around it before they begin.
