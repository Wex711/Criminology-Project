Crime Prevention

This website examines the causes of crime through both theoretical and applied perspectives. Rather than viewing crime solely through punishment and reaction, it explores how criminological research suggests that many crimes can be prevented by addressing their underlying causes before they occur.

When Crime Isn't Justifiable

This website's purpose is to explore the many factors that contribute to criminal behavior. Crime is often influenced by institutional, social, economic, and environmental conditions, but understanding these influences does not excuse criminal behavior.

While many crimes are socially constructed through law, many offenses also produce real physical, emotional, and financial harm or threaten the social order. Examining why crime occurs is not intended to justify offending, but rather to identify opportunities for prevention before crimes occur.

Crime Has Victims

Crime remains a significant issue in the United States, with approximately 1,325,584 violent crimes and 6,513,815 property crimes reported annually. This corresponds to a violent crime rate of 398.5 per 100,000 people and a property crime rate of 1,958.2 per 100,000 people.

Source: Federal Bureau of Investigation. (2020). Crime Data Explorer.

Crime Is Complex

Crime is often believed to be the product of individual decisions, and in modern society it is commonly assumed that people are solely responsible for their own actions. While individual choice certainly matters, criminological research shows that larger social forces also shape criminal behavior.

Social inequality, community conditions, institutional failures, economic opportunity, and cultural pressures all influence how individuals respond to adversity and approach problems. Understanding these influences provides a more complete explanation of crime than personal responsibility alone.

Classical Theory

Classical Theory argues that individuals possess free will and make rational decisions by weighing the potential benefits and costs of offending. Offenders consider factors such as the likelihood of success, the chances of being caught, and the severity of punishment before deciding whether to commit a crime. Cesare Beccaria and Jeremy Bentham were among the primary proponents of this perspective, arguing that crime is better prevented than punished.

"It is better to prevent crimes than to punish them."

— Cesare Beccaria (1764/1963)

Looking Beyond Individual Choice

While Classical Theory emphasizes rational decision-making, later criminological theories argue that those decisions are also shaped by broader social pressures. Cultural expectations, social inequality, institutional influences, anomie, and strain can all increase the likelihood that individuals turn toward crime.
